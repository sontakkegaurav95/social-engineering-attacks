# 🔐 Task 5: Research Report on Social Engineering Attacks

## 📌 Objective
The objective of this report is to study and understand social engineering attacks such as phishing, pretexting, and baiting. It explains how these attacks work, their real-world impact, and effective prevention techniques.

---

## 🌐 Introduction
Social engineering attacks are one of the most dangerous cybersecurity threats because they exploit **human psychology** rather than technical vulnerabilities. Attackers manipulate individuals into revealing confidential information such as passwords, banking details, or access credentials.

These attacks are widely used because they are easy to execute and often highly effective.

---

## ⚠️ Types of Social Engineering Attacks

### 1. Phishing

#### 🔹 Definition:
Phishing is a type of attack where attackers send fake emails or messages pretending to be from trusted sources.

#### 🔹 How it Works:
- Attacker sends a fake email (bank, company, etc.)
- Victim clicks on malicious link
- User enters sensitive data (passwords, OTP, etc.)
- Attacker steals information

#### 🔹 Types of Phishing:
- Email Phishing
- Spear Phishing (targeted)
- Whaling (targeting executives)
- Smishing (SMS phishing)

#### 🔹 Impact:
- Identity theft
- Financial loss
- Unauthorized access

#### 🔹 Case Study:
- Google and Facebook lost over $100 million due to a phishing scam between 2013–2015.

#### 🔹 Prevention:
- Verify sender email address
- Avoid clicking unknown links
- Use email filters
- Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

---

### 2. Pretexting

#### 🔹 Definition:
Pretexting involves creating a fake scenario (pretext) to trick individuals into providing sensitive information.

#### 🔹 How it Works:
- Attacker pretends to be a trusted authority (bank officer, IT support)
- Builds trust with the victim
- Requests confidential information

#### 🔹 Impact:
- Data breach
- Unauthorized access
- Loss of confidential information

#### 🔹 Case Study:
- Attackers impersonating company IT staff obtained employee credentials in multiple corporate breaches.

#### 🔹 Prevention:
- Verify identity before sharing information
- Do not disclose sensitive data over calls/emails
- Use internal verification protocols

---

### 3. Baiting

#### 🔹 Definition:
Baiting involves offering something attractive to lure victims into a trap.

#### 🔹 How it Works:
- Attacker leaves infected USB drives or fake downloads
- Victim uses the device or downloads file
- Malware is installed on the system

#### 🔹 Examples:
- Free movie/software downloads
- Infected USB drives labeled "Confidential"

#### 🔹 Impact:
- Malware infection
- Data theft
- System compromise

#### 🔹 Case Study:
- USB drop attacks where employees plug unknown USB devices into office computers.

#### 🔹 Prevention:
- Avoid unknown USB devices
- Download software from trusted sources
- Use antivirus software
- Disable autorun features

---

## 🧠 Why Social Engineering Works
- Exploits human emotions (fear, urgency, curiosity)
- Lack of cybersecurity awareness
- Trust in authority figures
- Poor security practices

---

## 🛡️ Preventive Measures

### 🔐 Technical Measures:
- Use firewalls and antivirus software
- Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Email filtering and spam detection
- Regular system updates

### 👨‍🏫 Human Awareness:
- Conduct cybersecurity training
- Educate employees about phishing and scams
- Encourage reporting suspicious activities

### 🏢 Organizational Measures:
- Implement strict security policies
- Use role-based access control
- Regular security audits

---

## 📊 Conclusion
Social engineering attacks are highly effective because they target human behavior rather than systems. Attacks like phishing, pretexting, and baiting can lead to serious financial and data losses. Organizations and individuals must combine **technical defenses and awareness training** to protect against such threats.
